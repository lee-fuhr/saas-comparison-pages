Best Roam Research alternatives

If you use Roam Research for networked thought, you know how powerful bi-directional linking can be for connecting ideas across disciplines. But maybe you are looking for a different pricing model, offline access, or a more structured workspace. This article covers three strong alternatives: Notion, Obsidian, and Logseq. Each one supports backlinks and graph views, but they differ in approach, cost, and workflow.

alternative

Notion

combines databases, notes, and project management into one app. Its backlinks work across pages, but the graph view is less visual than Roam’s. Notion is best for academics who also need tables, calendars, and team collaboration.

Try Notion →
alternative

Obsidian

stores all notes as plain Markdown files on your local computer. Its bi-directional links and graph view are very similar to Roam, and you can extend it with plugins for extra features. Obsidian is free for personal use and ideal if you value privacy and long-term file portability.

Try Obsidian →
alternative

Logseq

is an open-source tool that uses an outliner format like Roam. It offers bi-directional linking, block-level references, and a local-first, encrypted sync option. Logseq is a strong choice for thinkers who want the Roam experience without the subscription fee.

Try Logseq →

How they compare

- **Bi-directional linking**: All three support backlinks, but Obsidian and Logseq give you the most Roam-like graph and block references. Notion has backlinks but lacks a native block-level linking and a live graph. - **Pricing**: Obsidian and Logseq are free for local use (Logseq sync costs extra). Notion is free with limited storage and a paid plan for more features. Roam is subscription-only. - **Offline access**: Obsidian and Logseq work fully offline. Notion requires internet for syncing and editing, though it has limited offline caching. - **File format**: Obsidian and Logseq use plain Markdown files you own. Notion uses a proprietary database format. This affects portability and future-proofing.

Frequently asked questions

Can I import my Roam database into these alternatives?
Obsidian and Logseq have built-in importers for Roam JSON and Markdown exports. Notion also supports import but may require manual cleanup for block references. Exports from Roam are usually a good starting point, but expect some formatting adjustments.
Which tool is best for a PhD student managing dozens of linked sources?
Obsidian is popular for its folderless tagging, graph overview, and plugin ecosystem for citation management (e.g., Zotero integration). Logseq is another strong option if you prefer an outliner and want to keep everything local. Notion works well if you also need to organize databases of papers, tasks, and deadlines.
