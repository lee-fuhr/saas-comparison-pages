Choosing the right enterprise learning platform can feel overwhelming. Two of the most common contenders for corporate L&D teams are Docebo and LinkedIn Learning. Docebo is a full featured learning management system (LMS) built for administering, delivering, and tracking training at scale. LinkedIn Learning, on the other hand, is a content heavy platform with a massive library of video courses, often used as a supplement to an existing LMS. For L&D professionals evaluating these two, the core trade off is control versus convenience. Docebo gives you deep customization, compliance tracking, and authoring tools. LinkedIn Learning offers a polished, consumer grade experience with curated content but limited administrative capabilities. Below we explore the most common alternatives and a direct comparison to help you decide.

alternative

EdCast

. EdCast is a knowledge management and learning experience platform that focuses on content aggregation and personalized learning paths. It is best for organizations that want to pull in content from multiple sources (including internal and external) and use AI to recommend courses to employees, but it requires more integration work than an out of the box LMS.

alternative

Degreed

. Degreed positions itself as a skills platform that tracks all learning, not just formal courses. It aggregates content from providers like LinkedIn Learning and Coursera, and measures skill proficiency over time. It is ideal for companies that prioritize skill development and want a single place to log on the job learning, but it is less suited for heavy compliance training or instructor led courses. **360Learning**. 360Learning is a collaborative LMS that emphasizes peer to peer learning and course authoring by subject matter experts. It is a strong choice for organizations that want to quickly build internal training without a dedicated instructional design team. However, its content library is smaller than LinkedIn Learning’s, and it may not scale as smoothly for very large enterprises.

Skillsoft

. Skillsoft offers a broad content library including video courses, books, and certification prep, similar to LinkedIn Learning but with more depth in IT and business skills. It also provides a full LMS (Percipio) which combines content delivery with basic administration. It is a good middle ground for companies that want both a robust catalog and some admin control, but it can be less customizable than Docebo.

alternative

Coursera for Business

. Coursera for Business gives employees access to university style courses, professional certificates, and degree programs from top institutions. It excels at deep, cohort based learning and credentialing, but it is not a full LMS. L&D teams typically use it alongside another platform for compliance tracking and custom content.

How they compare

The key difference between Docebo and LinkedIn Learning is that Docebo is a complete LMS, while LinkedIn Learning is primarily a content subscription service. Docebo lets you create your own courses, set up compliance automations, and run detailed reports on completion and skill gaps. LinkedIn Learning offers a ready made library of 16,000 plus video courses but gives you minimal control over content structure, reporting depth, or custom workflows. For compliance heavy environments (safety, regulatory, onboarding), Docebo is the clear choice. For organizations that want a low effort, off the shelf learning resource for professional development (soft skills, software training, leadership), LinkedIn Learning is faster to deploy and easier for employees to use. In terms of cost, LinkedIn Learning is typically priced per user per month with no implementation fees, while Docebo’s pricing is higher and often based on active users plus add ons. Scale and integration also matter: Docebo plays well with HRIS and CRM systems, while LinkedIn Learning integrates mostly via content embedding in other LMSs. Most large enterprises end up using both: Docebo as the backbone and LinkedIn Learning as a content source inside it.
