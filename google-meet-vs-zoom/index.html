Where Google Meet wins

Google Meet integrates directly with Google Workspace. If your team already uses Gmail, Google Calendar, and Google Drive, Meet plugs into those tools without extra effort. You can create a meeting link from a Calendar event in one click, and every participant with a Google account can join from their browser without downloading anything. That simplicity reduces friction for teams that live in the Google ecosystem.

Meet also offers strong security by default. All meetings are encrypted in transit, and Google enforces strict access controls for Google Workspace accounts. For organizations that need compliance with standards like HIPAA or FedRAMP, Meet’s enterprise-level security is a clear advantage. Additionally, Meet includes live captions powered by Google’s speech recognition, which works well in English and several other languages. The interface is clean and minimal, which can be a plus for teams that want fewer distractions during calls.

Another win for Google Meet versus Zoom is its straightforward pricing. For existing Google Workspace subscribers, Meet is included at no extra cost. Even the free tier of Google Workspace allows group meetings up to sixty minutes. For teams that already pay for Google services, choosing Meet avoids an additional subscription.

Where Zoom wins

Zoom is famous for its feature-rich experience, especially in large meetings and webinars. It offers breakout rooms, virtual backgrounds, polls, and a robust chat panel, features that Google Meet either lacks or handles less smoothly. If your team runs interactive workshops, training sessions, or all‑hands meetings that need breakout discussions, Zoom versus Google Meet is an easy win for Zoom.

Zoom also excels in third‑party integrations. It connects with hundreds of apps through the Zoom App Marketplace, including Slack, Microsoft Teams, Salesforce, and many project management tools. This flexibility matters for teams that rely on a diverse software stack. In addition, Zoom’s recording and transcription options are polished. You can record locally or to the cloud, with searchable transcripts and automatic highlights.

For larger audiences, Zoom’s webinar and large meeting plans support up to one thousand participants (and more with add‑ons). Google Meet caps standard meetings at one hundred for most plans and two hundred fifty for certain enterprise tiers, but Zoom’s ceiling is higher for less money in some cases. Zoom also provides a more customizable waiting room and meeting controls, giving hosts finer‑grained control over who enters and what they can do.

Verdict

The choice between Google Meet and Zoom comes down to your team’s existing infrastructure and meeting style. If your team is fully embedded in Google Workspace, values simplicity and security, and does not need advanced breakout features, Google Meet is the natural choice. It costs you nothing extra and removes the hassle of switching between logins.

If your team needs rich interactivity, breakout rooms, extensive third‑party integrations, larger audiences, or polished webinar tools, Zoom is the stronger option. Zoom versus Google Meet is a trade‑off between seamless integration and advanced features. For most teams, the best approach is to test both with a trial. Either platform will handle everyday video calls well, but the one that aligns with your team’s workflow will make your meetings run smoother.
