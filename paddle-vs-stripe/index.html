Paddle vs Stripe: which should you pick?

If you are a SaaS founder choosing between payment providers, the Paddle vs Stripe decision often comes down to one key difference: who acts as the merchant of record. Paddle takes on that role, meaning it handles sales tax, VAT, invoicing, and fraud liability for you. Stripe is a pure payment processor, leaving you responsible for those compliance tasks. Both can handle recurring subscriptions and global payments, but the trade-offs around control versus convenience matter deeply for different business models. This article compares Paddle versus Stripe in plain terms, focusing on where each platform wins for SaaS companies.

Where Paddle wins

Paddle wins when you want to offload tax and compliance headaches. As the merchant of record, Paddle calculates, collects, and remits sales tax and VAT in over 50 countries. You never have to worry about registering for tax IDs or filing returns. This is a huge time saver for early stage SaaS teams or solo founders who cannot afford a tax expert.

Paddle also handles invoicing and receipts automatically, including localized billing in multiple currencies. If your customers require specific invoice formats or have complex tax exemption needs, Paddle takes care of it. Another win: Paddle absorbs fraud liability. If a chargeback occurs, Paddle covers the loss, not you. This removes a major risk for high volume or high risk subscription businesses.

Finally, Paddle’s unified reporting simplifies revenue recognition. You get one dashboard showing net revenue after Paddle’s fees and taxes, which makes accounting simpler compared to Stripe where you must reconcile multiple tax lines manually.

Where Stripe wins

Stripe wins on flexibility and control. You are not locked into Paddle’s pricing or tax handling. Stripe charges a simple per transaction fee (typically 2.9% plus a fixed fee for domestic cards), which can be lower than Paddle’s blended fee (which includes the cost of being the merchant of record). For high volume SaaS companies, these savings add up.

Stripe also offers deeper customization. You can build your own checkout flow, use Stripe Elements to design a unique payment page, and access advanced features like Stripe Billing with custom invoice schedules, subscription proration, and metered billing. Paddle’s checkout is more rigid, which can frustrate teams that want a tailored user experience.

Another win: Stripe’s global reach is broader for payment methods. It supports hundreds of local payment options (like Alipay, Bancontact, or iDEAL) compared to Paddle’s smaller set. If your SaaS sells to countries where customers prefer non card payments, Stripe gives you more ways to convert them.

Stripe also integrates with more tools. Its API and webhooks are industry standard, and most accounting, CRM, and analytics platforms connect directly to Stripe. Paddle’s integrations exist but are fewer. For technical teams, Stripe’s documentation and developer experience are superior.

Verdict

Choose Paddle versus Stripe based on your team size and your willingness to handle compliance. If you are a small or early stage SaaS with limited resources, Paddle’s all in one merchant of record model is the safer bet. You avoid tax traps, fraud liability, and billing complexity. It is worth the higher fee for the peace of mind.

Choose Stripe if you have a larger team, a tax or legal expert on staff, or a need for full control over your checkout flow and pricing. Stripe’s lower fees and superior customization make it the better choice for scaling SaaS companies that can manage their own tax compliance. If you plan to expand into many countries with diverse payment methods, Stripe gives you the flexibility to adapt.

In short: Paddle for convenience and compliance outsourcing; Stripe for control and cost savings. Both are excellent tools, but the right pick depends on whether you want to build your own compliance system or pay someone else to own it.
