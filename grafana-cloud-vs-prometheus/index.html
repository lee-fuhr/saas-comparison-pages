Grafana Cloud vs Prometheus: which should you pick?

When observability engineers evaluate managed monitoring tools, the choice between Grafana Cloud and Prometheus often comes down to a classic trade-off: a fully hosted platform versus a self-managed open-source stack. Grafana Cloud is a SaaS offering that bundles metrics (powered by Grafana Mimir, a Prometheus-compatible backend), logs (Loki), traces (Tempo), and dashboards into one service. Prometheus, on the other hand, is the widely adopted open-source monitoring system that teams deploy and operate themselves. This article compares Grafana Cloud versus Prometheus directly, focusing on what each option does best for engineers who need to keep an eye on infrastructure and applications.

Where Grafana Cloud wins

Grafana Cloud removes almost all operational overhead. You do not need to provision servers, manage Prometheus retention, handle compactions, or worry about high availability. The platform automatically scales ingestion and storage so you can send metrics without capacity planning. For teams that lack dedicated SREs or platform engineers, this is a major time saver.

Another strong point is the integrated observability stack. Grafana Cloud provides Prometheus-compatible metrics alongside logs and traces in a single interface. You can correlate a spike in HTTP errors with a log line and a trace without stitching together separate tools. Alerting is also built in and works across all data types. Setting up a new team member is as simple as inviting them – no need to configure Grafana or Prometheus servers.

Cost predictability is also a win. Grafana Cloud charges based on the amount of metrics, logs, and traces you send. There is a free tier for small workloads, and you can scale up without paying for idle compute. For organizations that do not want to manage infrastructure and prefer a single vendor relationship, Grafana Cloud versus self-hosted Prometheus is an easy call.

Where Prometheus wins

Prometheus gives you total control. You decide where to run it, how to configure scrapers, what retention policy to set, and how to handle data durability. There is no vendor lock-in: you can swap the backend, move to Thanos or VictoriaMetrics, or back up your TSDB to object storage. For teams with strict compliance or data residency rules, keeping metrics inside your own network is a requirement that Prometheus meets without compromise.

Cost can also favor Prometheus – the software itself is free. You only pay for the servers and storage you provision. If you already have spare capacity or can run it on Kubernetes with persistent volumes, the total cost of ownership may be lower than a managed service, especially at high ingestion volumes. Prometheus also has a vast ecosystem of exporters, integrations, and community knowledge. Troubleshooting a custom metric source is often easier when you can inspect the raw scrape target.

Flexibility is another advantage. Prometheus versus Grafana Cloud becomes relevant when you need to run custom relabeling, federation, or complex service discovery that goes beyond what a SaaS platform exposes. You can also keep data for years if you can afford the storage – a feature that Grafana Cloud pricing typically discourages.

Verdict

Neither Grafana Cloud nor Prometheus is universally better for observability engineers. The right choice depends on your team’s capacity and constraints. Choose Grafana Cloud if you want a turnkey managed experience, integrated logs and traces, and no operational burden. It is ideal for teams that are small, fast-moving, or prefer to outsource infrastructure. Choose Prometheus if you need full control, have strict data governance, or already run a mature Kubernetes or bare-metal environment with the skills to operate it. Prometheus versus Grafana Cloud is also a matter of budget: if you have spare compute and want to avoid variable monthly costs, self-hosting wins. Many organizations start with Prometheus and later migrate to Grafana Cloud as they grow, or they run a hybrid model where self-hosted Prometheus feeds into Grafana Cloud for dashboards. Ultimately, both are valid approaches – the verdict is to evaluate your operational maturity, compliance needs, and long-term scaling plans before deciding.
