Zapier vs Make: which should you pick?

If your team is building automated workflows without writing code, you have likely come across two leading tools: Zapier and Make (formerly Integromat). Both let you connect apps and automate repetitive tasks, but they take different approaches. Zapier focuses on simplicity and a vast library of pre-built integrations, while Make emphasizes visual flexibility and advanced logic. For no-code teams deciding between Zapier versus Make, the choice comes down to what matters more: ease of getting started or depth of control.

Where Zapier wins

Zapier is built for speed and simplicity. Its interface guides you step by step through creating a “Zap” (the name for a workflow). You pick a trigger app and an action app, map a few fields, and the automation runs. This makes it ideal for no-code teams that want to set up common automations fast, like sending a Slack message when a new lead comes in from a form, or adding a row to a spreadsheet from an email.

Zapier also has an enormous collection of apps. With over 7,000 integrations, it covers almost every popular business tool your team might use. If you need to connect a niche SaaS product, Zapier likely already supports it. Its pre-built “Zap templates” are another strength: your team can find a ready-to-use workflow for hundreds of use cases without starting from scratch.

For teams that need reliability and support, Zapier offers better uptime and a more mature customer support system. If an automation fails, Zapier’s error handling is straightforward, and its paid plans include features like multiple steps, filters, and path routing (called “Paths”) without requiring you to learn a visual canvas.

Where Make wins

Make (formerly Integromat) is more flexible and powerful for complex workflows. Instead of a linear sequence of steps, Make uses a visual “scenario” builder where you drag and drop modules on a canvas. This lets you create branching logic, loops, sub-scenarios, and data transformations that are hard or impossible to do in Zapier’s linear flow.

For no-code teams building automations that involve data manipulation, like splitting text, converting formats, or aggregating results, Make provides built-in tools (aggregators, iterators, routers) that Zapier lacks natively. You can also store and reuse data across steps with variables and custom modules.

Make’s pricing is another advantage for teams that run many automations. Its free tier is more generous than Zapier’s, and paid plans are often less expensive per task compared to Zapier’s per-task pricing. For high-volume workflows, Make versus Zapier usually means significantly lower costs.

Make also excels at handling webhooks and custom API calls. If your no-code team needs to connect to an app without a dedicated Zapier integration, you can build a module using a raw HTTP request or webhook in Make. This unlocks almost any service without waiting for an official integration.

Verdict

For no-code teams, the choice between Zapier versus Make depends on your automation complexity and team size. Choose Zapier if you value speed, simplicity, and a huge library of ready‑to‑use integrations. It is best for teams that want to automate common tasks with minimal learning curve and don’t need advanced logic.

Choose Make if your workflows require conditional branching, data manipulation, or custom API connections, and if you need to control costs as your automation volume grows. Make rewards the extra learning time with more power and flexibility.

In short, Zapier gets you running in minutes; Make lets you build what you want. Evaluate your team’s most frequent use cases and pick the tool that matches them best.
