If your company is looking for an open-source metadata catalog built on Neo4j, Amundsen is the natural starting point. It uses the Neo4j graph database to model relationships between datasets, tables, columns, and users, making lineage and discovery fast and intuitive. Amundsen is free to deploy, highly customizable, and backed by a strong open-source community. Its core design philosophy centers on making data discovery as simple as a search engine, which fits teams that want a lightweight, developer-friendly solution without vendor lock-in.

alternative

Alation

. A commercial data catalog focused on data intelligence and collaboration. It offers automated metadata extraction, data profiling, and a rich user interface for business users. Unlike Amundsen, Alation does not use Neo4j and comes with a subscription cost.

alternative

Collibra

. A comprehensive data intelligence platform that includes catalog, governance, and compliance features. It targets large enterprises with complex regulatory needs. Collibra is proprietary, expensive, and does not use Neo4j, making it a heavy alternative to Amundsen’s lightweight open-source approach.

alternative

Atlan

. A modern, cloud-based data catalog that emphasizes collaboration and ease of use. It provides out-of-the-box integrations and a polished UI, but it is not open-source and runs on its own infrastructure. Atlan is a good fit for teams that want a managed solution without the overhead of self-hosting.

alternative

DataHub

. An open-source metadata platform originally built at LinkedIn. It uses a variety of storage backends (not Neo4j by default) and offers strong support for real-time metadata ingestion. DataHub is more complex to deploy than Amundsen but provides advanced lineage features and a larger ecosystem of connectors.

alternative

Secoda

. A lightweight, cloud-based data catalog that focuses on simplicity and quick setup. It is proprietary and offers a free tier for small teams. Secoda does not use Neo4j and is best suited for startups or small teams that want minimal configuration, not for those specifically seeking a Neo4j-based open-source catalog.

How they compare

When comparing Amundsen vs Collibra, the key differences come down to openness, architecture, and cost. Amundsen is open-source and built on Neo4j, giving you full control over your graph-based metadata model and no licensing fees. Collibra is a closed-source enterprise platform with a high price tag and a different underlying data model. Amundsen is ideal for technical teams that prioritize customizability and transparency, while Collibra suits organizations that need out-of-the-box governance and regulatory compliance features. For companies specifically seeking an open-source catalog on Neo4j, Amundsen is the clear choice; Collibra cannot match that combination.

Frequently asked questions

Why is Neo4j important for a metadata catalog like Amundsen?
Neo4j stores metadata as a graph, which makes it easy to trace data lineage from source to report and visualize relationships between tables, columns, and teams. This graph structure is more intuitive for discovery than traditional relational databases, and it allows Amundsen to return fast search results even as your metadata grows.
Can I use Amundsen if my company already has Collibra?
Yes, but it would require a separate deployment and integration effort. Amundsen and Collibra are not interoperable out of the box. If your goal is to replace Collibra with an open-source alternative on Neo4j, Amundsen is a strong candidate. Keep in mind that you will lose Collibra’s built-in governance workflows and support.
