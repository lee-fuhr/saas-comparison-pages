Best Logseq alternatives

Logseq is a popular open-source knowledge base that stores your data locally as plain Markdown or Org-mode files. It appeals to users who value privacy, offline access, and community-driven development. However, some users may want a different workflow, more mature plugin ecosystems, or features like block-level referencing that other tools offer. This article covers three notable alternatives for open-source enthusiasts seeking a local-first knowledge base.

alternative

Obsidian

stores your notes as plain Markdown files on your local device, giving you full control over your data. It offers a rich plugin system and strong linking features, though its core app is not open source. For open-source enthusiasts, Obsidian is a good choice if you prioritize flexibility and community plugins over pure open-source licensing.

alternative

Roam Research

pioneered block-level references and a networked thought approach, but it is a proprietary, cloud-based service. Data is stored on Roam’s servers rather than locally, which may conflict with local-first preferences. That said, its outliner interface and bidirectional linking are powerful for long-form thinking.

alternative

Notion

provides a versatile workspace combining notes, databases, and project management, but it is entirely cloud-based and proprietary. Your data is stored on Notion’s servers with no local-first option, and its source code is not open. Notion is best for those who need an all-in-one collaboration platform despite the lack of local storage and open-source principles.

How they compare

Logseq is fully open source and local-first with block-level editing, similar to Roam but with offline storage. Obsidian offers local Markdown files and extensive customization but is not open source. Roam Research provides powerful outliner features but is cloud-only and proprietary. Notion excels at databases and team collaboration but sacrifices local control and open-source transparency. For open-source enthusiasts who want local-first, Logseq remains the strongest match, while Obsidian is a close second for privacy advocates who can accept a proprietary core.

Frequently asked questions

Is Obsidian open source?
No, Obsidian’s core application is closed source, but its plugin system and community are active. Many plugins are open source, and the Markdown files are fully portable.
Can I self-host Roam Research or Notion?
Neither Roam Research nor Notion supports self-hosting. They are cloud-only services. For self-hosted options, consider Logseq or other open-source tools like Trilium or Joplin.
