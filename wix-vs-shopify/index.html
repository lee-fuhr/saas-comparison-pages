Wix vs Shopify: which should you pick?

If you are a small business owner building your first online store, you have probably heard two names mentioned more than any others: Wix versus Shopify. Both platforms let you create a website and sell products, but they take very different approaches. Wix started as a general website builder and added store features. Shopify was built from the ground up for ecommerce. This comparison is designed to help you decide which one matches your goals, your technical comfort level, and your budget.

Where Wix wins

Wix shines when you want complete control over the look and feel of your site without needing to know code. Its drag-and-drop editor is more flexible than Shopify’s. You can place a product image where you want it, move a text box an inch to the left, or add a video right next to a “buy now” button. Shopify works more like a template system where most content is locked into rows and columns.

Wix also wins on design freedom for non-product pages. If you run a small bakery that wants a photo gallery, a blog, and a menu alongside a store, Wix makes that seamless. Shopify treats product pages as the main event and leaves you with fewer styling options for the rest of your site.

For cost-conscious beginners, Wix offers a much lower entry point. You can start with a basic website plan and add the store features when you are ready. The free tier lets you explore the builder without paying. Shopify has no free plan and requires a monthly subscription before you can add a single product.

Wix also gives you more generous storage limits on its lower-tier plans. If you plan to sell high resolution images or digital downloads, this matters more than you might think.

Where Shopify wins

Shopify wins every time the conversation turns to selling. Its checkout process is faster, more secure, and converts more visitors into buyers than Wix. Shopify Payments handles credit cards without adding extra transaction fees, while Wix charges its own fee on top of payment processor fees unless you use Wix Payments. Shopify’s abandoned cart recovery feature is built in and effective. Wix offers a similar tool, but it is less powerful.

Inventory management is another clear win for Shopify. If you have more than a handful of products, or if you ever plan to sell the same item in different colors and sizes, Shopify’s system keeps everything organized. You can track stock across multiple locations, set low stock alerts, and manage purchase orders. Wix’s inventory system works fine for a small number of products but struggles as you grow.

Shopify also wins for SEO. Its product pages automatically generate clean URLs, proper heading structures, and fast loading speeds. Wix has improved its SEO in recent years, but Shopify still ranks higher for product searches more consistently.

When you want to scale, Shopify’s app store offers thousands of plugins for shipping, marketing, accounting, and customer service. Wix has an app market too, but it is smaller and the integrations are less robust. If you think you might need a shipping calculator, a loyalty points program, or a Facebook Shop integration, Shopify’s ecosystem is more mature.

Verdict

Choose Wix if your online store is a small part of a larger website, if you are a hands-on visual designer who wants total control, and if you are testing the waters with a tiny budget and a low number of products. Wix is perfect for a boutique, a hobby shop, or a service business that also sells a few items.

Choose Shopify if your primary business is selling products, if you have a hundred or more SKUs, or if you plan to grow quickly. Shopify handles the heavy lifting of transactions, shipping, and inventory so you can focus on marketing and fulfillment. It costs more upfront, but it saves time and lost sales in the long run.

For most small business owners starting their first online store, Shopify is the safer bet. The learning curve is slightly steeper, but the platform is designed to help you make sales. Wix is easier to start with, but you may find yourself rebuilding on Shopify later if your store takes off. Decide based on whether you want a beautiful site first or a selling machine first.
