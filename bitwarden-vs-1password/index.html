Bitwarden vs 1Password: which should you pick?

For privacy-conscious users, choosing a password manager is a serious decision. Two of the most popular options are Bitwarden and 1Password. Bitwarden is fully open source, meaning its code can be inspected by anyone. 1Password is proprietary but has a strong reputation for security and usability. This article compares Bitwarden versus 1Password to help you decide which one aligns better with your privacy values.

Where Bitwarden wins

Bitwarden is open source. This is its biggest advantage for privacy-conscious users. Anyone can review the code, audit the encryption, and verify that no hidden telemetry or backdoors exist. Bitwarden also offers self-hosting: you can run the server on your own hardware, giving you full control over your data. No third party sees your vault. Even if you use their cloud, you can trust that the code does what it claims.

Bitwarden is cheaper. Its premium plan costs very little and includes features like TOTP (time-based one-time passwords) and emergency access. The free tier is generous and has no limits on devices. You can also use Bitwarden without any subscription at all if you self-host.

Security audits are public. Bitwarden has completed independent security audits and publishes the results. The encryption is standard AES-256 with PBKDF2 or Argon2. Because the code is open, the community can spot flaws quickly.

Bitwarden is also cross-platform with excellent browser extensions, mobile apps, and desktop apps. The interface is clean but less polished than 1Password. For users who prioritize transparency over design, Bitwarden is the clear winner.

Where 1Password wins

1Password is not open source, but it takes a different approach to privacy. It uses a Secret Key that is generated locally and never leaves your device. Even if 1Password’s servers were compromised, an attacker cannot decrypt your vault without that key. This means 1Password cannot access your data even if legally compelled. Bitwarden’s cloud service could technically be forced to decrypt user data if the master password were obtained, though Bitwarden encrypts everything client-side.

1Password has a more polished user experience. Its interface is intuitive, with features like inline filling, watchtower alerts for compromised passwords, and a travel mode that lets you remove sensitive items from your device when crossing borders. The secret key system also simplifies account recovery without relying on a master password hint.

For families and teams, 1Password offers better sharing controls and guest accounts. It also supports biometric unlock on more platforms. The desktop app is robust and works offline well.

While 1Password has not released its source code, it has undergone independent security audits and is a trusted name in the industry. Some privacy-conscious users prefer its “zero knowledge” architecture with the secret key over an open source model that relies on the provider’s infrastructure.

Verdict

For privacy-conscious users, Bitwarden versus 1Password comes down to a trade-off between transparency and convenience. If you want complete control and the ability to inspect every line of code, choose Bitwarden. Its open source nature and self-hosting option give you the highest possible assurance that your data is handled correctly. It also costs less and still offers excellent security.

If you prefer a more polished tool with a unique secret key that adds an extra layer of protection against server compromise, and you trust the company’s security reputation, 1Password is a strong choice. Both are top-tier password managers that protect your privacy. Your final decision should be based on whether open source transparency or a proven closed-source system with a secret key matters more to you.
